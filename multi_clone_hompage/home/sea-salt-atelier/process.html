The Method

Ocean to Crystal in Five Stages

The Gradient System

Five Stages of Tidal Alchemy

1
Stage One — Spring Tide

Deep Ocean Water Selection

We intake seawater exclusively during spring tides — the highest tidal events of the lunar cycle — when ocean currents draw water up from deeper, more mineralically dense layers. At the Atlantic site, intake occurs at dawn on the three days of maximum spring tide, typically in June or July.

Spring tides occur approximately twice per lunar month. We use only the highest 3 days per year.
2
Stage Two — Weeks 1–3

Sequential Twelve-Pond Concentration

Water moves by gravity through twelve evaporation ponds, each maintained at a progressively higher salinity. Early ponds precipitate out calcium and magnesium carbonates — desirable removal. The remaining brine, by pond seven, has concentrated its sodium, potassium, and trace minerals into an increasingly saturated solution approaching 25% salinity.

Typical progression time: 18–25 days, weather dependent
3
Stage Three — The Wait

Solar Crystallisation in the Final Pond

When brine reaches 300g/L salinity in the final pond, crystallisation begins. We monitor the surface daily. Ideal conditions — temperature above 28°C, humidity below 65%, wind from the west — produce flat, pyramidal fleur de sel crystals on the surface. We do not intervene. We do not shade, fan, or heat the ponds. We wait.

Crystallisation window: 7–21 days. Timing determined by crystal quality under hand lens, not calendar.
4
Stage Four — Dawn Harvest

Hand-Raking Before the Heat

Harvest begins at 5:30am. The paludier enters the pond on wooden walkways and uses a 3-metre wooden rake to draw surface crystals towards a central collection point in a single, slow, even motion. The fleur de sel — the finest crystals from the very surface — is skimmed separately by hand using a traditional lousse, a flat wooden paddle. Each movement is unhurried. There is no quota.

Harvest window: approximately 90 minutes per pond, per day
5
Stage Five — Atelier

Air Drying, Sorting, and Hand-Packing

Harvested crystals are spread on slate drying beds in the open air for 24–48 hours. Sorting follows: each batch is examined under a 10x hand lens, with irregular formations removed and oversized clusters broken by hand. Porcelain vessels are filled on a precision scale, sealed with corks and natural resin, and labelled with the harvest date, pond number, and paludier's personal mark.

Average per-vessel handling time: 6 minutes
Tools of the Paludier

Instruments of Restraint

⊕

The Ladoue Rake

A 3-metre wooden rake with a flat, wide-toothed stainless head, used for drawing coarse salt from pond bottoms. Custom-made in our workshop to match the specific pond dimensions of each site.

◡

The Lousse

A flat, curved wooden paddle used exclusively for skimming fleur de sel from the water surface. Must be moved at 2cm per second maximum — any faster disturbs the surface and breaks the crystals.

▣

Slate Drying Beds

Traditional Breton slate tiles, slightly angled, on which freshly harvested crystals dry in the coastal breeze. Slate is chosen for its thermal neutrality — it neither heats nor cools the crystals during drying.

◎

Porcelain Vessels

Hand-thrown porcelain vessels, fired in Limoges, with a natural celadon interior glaze. Chosen for mineral neutrality — no lead, no barium. Salt stored in porcelain never tastes of its container.
