The Method

Botanical Pressing

The Practice

The Moment of Collection Is Everything.

Botanical pressing is a practice of attention. The decision of when to collect — which flower, at what stage of development, at what hour of the day — determines the outcome more than any technical decision made afterwards. We have refined every aspect of our pressing technique over seven years. But the moment of collection remains the skill that cannot be systematised. It requires knowing what a plant looks like at the precise second of its best self.

Five Stages

From Field to Frame

I

Field Survey and Collection

Collection begins with a field survey — a visit to the location at the appropriate season to assess what is flowering and at what stage. We do not collect on the first visit. We observe, sketch, and return when the specimens are at their best. Collection is done with sharp scissors at the base of the stem, early in the morning after any dew has dried but before the midday heat. Each specimen is placed immediately in blotting paper. We never carry specimens loosely.

Observation before collection
II

Identification and Recording

Every specimen is identified to species level before pressing begins. For uncommon specimens or unfamiliar species, we consult reference herbaria and botanical keys. The collection data — date, location (with GPS coordinates for all collections from 2022 onward), weather conditions, and brief field notes — is recorded in the atelier log before the specimen enters the press. This record becomes the basis of the specimen card.

Scientific ID required · GPS from 2022
III

Preparation and Placement

The specimen is prepared for pressing within 30 minutes of collection. Preparation involves making any necessary adjustments to the natural position of petals or leaves — coaxing a petal that has folded in transit back to its natural position, or carefully separating overlapping elements so each feature is visible. The specimen is placed on folded blotting paper, covered with a second sheet, and loaded into the botanical press. The press is tightened incrementally over the first 24 hours as moisture is released.

Preparation within 30 minutes
IV

The Press Duration

Press durations at HERBA CARTA range from 14 to 42 days depending on the water content of the specimen. Thin-petalled flowers (poppies, anemones) require shorter durations — too long in the press and the petals become brittle. Thick, fleshy specimens (succulents, some orchids) require extended durations and intermittent blotting paper changes. We do not use heat to accelerate drying. Time is part of the process.

14–42 days · No heat acceleration
V

Mounting and Framing

The pressed specimen is mounted on pH-neutral cotton rag board using methylcellulose paste — the same adhesive used in museum conservation. The composition is considered for each individual piece: the natural form of the specimen determines the compositional approach, not a house style. Once dry, the piece is covered with museum anti-reflection glass and framed in solid wood. The specimen card is mounted on the reverse. Each piece is signed and numbered on the back of the frame.

Archival materials only · Museum glass
The Studio

A Field Journal and a Botanical Press.

The HERBA CARTA studio in Bath is a working space, not a showroom. It contains a botanical reference library of approximately 400 volumes, a collection of Victorian herbarium sheets used for study, and a working archive of every specimen produced since 2018.

The equipment is simple. Botanical presses of various sizes. Blotting paper. Good scissors. A copy of Stace's New Flora of the British Isles. A field journal going back seven years. The sophistication is in the practice, not the tools.

  • Traditional wooden botanical presses, custom-made in various formats from single-specimen to large-format
  • Acid-free blotting paper, changed on a defined schedule during long press durations
  • Methylcellulose paste for archival mounting — reversible without damage
  • Conservation-grade cotton rag board in three weights: 200, 300, and 500gsm
  • Museum anti-reflection glass with UV filtration for all framed work
  • Solid oak and walnut framing stock, finished with pure beeswax only
  • Archival inkjet printing on cotton rag for specimen cards
