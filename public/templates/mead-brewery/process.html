The Craft

How Aurelum is Made

Seven months of patience.
Not a single shortcut.

Aurelum is made according to a six-stage process that has not changed since the first batch in 2011. We do not use commercial yeast nutrients, do not fine with gelatin, and do not carbonate artificially. Every intervention is written down and weighed against a simple question: does this serve the honey? Usually, the answer is to do nothing at all. The most important tool in the cellar is restraint.

Six Stages
01
Stage One

Apiary Selection

Each batch begins the previous spring, when we visit the named apiaries and evaluate the season's potential. Aurelum works with three permanent apiaries — Mas del Llum in Catalonia, Hive Alp in the Haute-Alpes, and Duna Méhészet in the Hungarian Great Plain. Selection is based on flora diversity, colony health records, and beekeeper relationship. If a season is poor, that apiary does not contribute to the vintage.

Timeline: April – June · Yield assessment
02
Stage Two

Must Preparation

Raw honey is diluted to a gravity of between 1.115 and 1.135 using spring water drawn from a single aquifer beneath the cellar. No heat above 35°C is applied during must preparation — excessive warmth volatilises the aromatic compounds that make each apiary distinct. Dilution is done slowly, over two days, with continuous gentle stirring. Wild yeast from the honey surface is the primary fermentation driver.

Timeline: September · 2-day preparation window
03
Stage Three

Primary Fermentation

Fermentation takes place in 600-litre sandstone vessels that have been in the cellar since the property was built in 1864. The stone maintains a constant 14°C during autumn without refrigeration. Fermentation is slow — typically 45 to 60 days — and produces a mead of unusual depth because the yeast is never stressed by temperature fluctuation. No added sulphites. Minimal racking.

Timeline: October – November · 45–60 days
04
Stage Four

Botanical Addition

For botanical expressions only, whole dried botanicals — never extracts or tinctures — are introduced to the vessel after primary fermentation is complete and the mead has been racked once. Each botanical parcel is sourced by name, assayed for potency, and added in quantities determined by nose and by record of what previous batches have taught us. The mead remains in contact with botanicals for between twelve and twenty-one days.

Timeline: December · 12–21 day contact
05
Stage Five

Cellar Aging

All Aurelum meads age for a minimum of twelve months in the sandstone cellar before evaluation. Reserve expressions age for no less than thirty-six months. Aging in stone rather than oak is a deliberate choice: it allows the honey's character to develop without imparting additional tannin or woody notes. The cellar's natural humidity keeps the vessels at near-constant conditions. Losses to evaporation are topped with the same honey-water must from each vintage.

Timeline: January – September · Minimum 12 months
06
Stage Six

Bottling & Allocation

Bottling is done by hand at the cellar, in one continuous session per batch to ensure consistency. Each bottle is filled by gravity, sealed with a natural cork, and hand-labelled with the batch name, apiary source, and bottling date. No disgorgement, no fining, no filtration. Bottles are numbered and allocated to the tasting list in order of registration. No batch has ever been sold through retail or distributed commercially.

Timeline: October · 400–600 bottles per batch
Raw Materials

Honey, water, time.
Nothing added that does not belong.

Every ingredient that enters an Aurelum vessel is documented by source, date, and quantity. The ingredient list for a traditional batch contains four items: honey, spring water, wild yeast cultures from the honey surface, and time. Botanical expressions add one further category — whole dried botanicals — sourced by name from single-farm suppliers.

  • I

    Raw Varietal Honey

    Named apiary, single flora where possible. Never blended across apiaries within one batch. Delivered within 6 weeks of extraction.

  • II

    Aquifer Spring Water

    Drawn from the artesian source beneath the cellar at a consistent 11°C. Mineral content measured monthly. No treatment beyond natural filtration.

  • III

    Wild Yeast

    Native to the honey surface of each apiary. No inoculation with commercial yeast strains. Fermentation pace is dictated entirely by the native microbiology.

  • IV

    Whole Dried Botanicals

    Used only in botanical expressions. Sourced from named single farms. Never as tinctures or extracts. Added whole, removed whole.

The Vessels

Sandstone Fermentation Vessels

The cellar contains fourteen sandstone vessels, the oldest dating to approximately 1820. The porous microstructure of the stone creates a slow, almost imperceptible exchange with the surrounding environment that cannot be replicated in stainless steel or glass. This micro-oxygenation, entirely passive, is part of why Aurelum meads develop more complexity with cellar time than most meads made in inert vessels.

Each vessel is washed by hand between batches using only hot water and a natural bristle brush. Chemical sanitisers have never been used.

The Bottling Equipment

Aurelum uses a gravity-fed bottling arm of no particular mechanical sophistication. The mead moves by its own weight from vessel to bottle. The work is slow — a full batch of 500 bottles takes one day — and this deliberateness is considered part of the process, not a limitation to be engineered around.
