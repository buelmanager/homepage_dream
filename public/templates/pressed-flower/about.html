About Herba Carta

"A pressed flower is not a dead thing. It is a moment — captured at the precise second of maximum aliveness, and kept there. Not preserved. Arrested."

Clara Whitmore, Founder, HERBA CARTA — Bath, 2018
The Beginning

Each Specimen, Once Living. Now Eternal.

HERBA CARTA was founded in Bath, Somerset in 2018 by Clara Whitmore, a botanical illustrator who had spent twelve years producing scientific drawings for university herbaria. She became preoccupied with the question of what happened to the specimens themselves — the actual plants that were pressed and mounted in herbarium sheets, catalogued, cross-referenced, and then rarely looked at again. She found them beautiful.

The atelier began as a refusal to treat botanical pressing as craft — as something decorative, domestic, or casual. HERBA CARTA treats pressed flowers as specimens: collected at the peak of their living moment, documented with the precision of natural history, and presented as objects worthy of the same consideration as drawing or photography.

Every piece leaves the atelier with a specimen card recording the plant's common and Latin name, the date and location of collection, the press duration, and the archival board weight. This is not a stylistic decision. It is the acknowledgment that what you are looking at is a real thing, from a real place, at a real moment in time. It has a provenance. It happened.

The Approach

Three Principles. No Compromise.

I

Specimen First

Every piece begins with the botanical specimen, not with a design intention. We do not collect plants to fit a planned composition. We collect plants that present themselves at their moment of maximum quality, and then build the composition around what the specimen offers. The plant leads. The arrangement follows.

II

Scientific Documentation

Every specimen is identified to species level, and the identification is documented on the specimen card. We do not use common names alone. If we cannot identify a specimen with confidence, we do not present it for sale. The scientific name is part of what the piece is — the record of what was alive in a particular place on a particular day.

III

Archival Materials Only

Every element that touches the specimen — board, backing, adhesive, glass, frame — is archival quality. No acid. No off-gassing. No synthetic colourants in the backing board. We use the same materials standard in natural history museum conservation. A HERBA CARTA piece should be presentable in a hundred years in the same condition it left our studio.

The Chronicle

Herba Carta, From the Start

2018

First Collection

Clara Whitmore begins pressing specimens from the countryside surrounding Bath, using the herbarium pressing techniques she learned in her botanical illustration work. The first twelve pieces are given to friends. She documents each one with a specimen card. A conversation with a friend who works in London gallery sales changes the direction of the project from personal practice to something more deliberate.

2019

The Archive Begins

HERBA CARTA opens with a collection of 40 specimens, each presented individually in conservation-grade frames with full scientific documentation. The first collection sells out within six weeks entirely through word of mouth. A decision is made never to restock a sold specimen — each piece in the HERBA CARTA catalogue represents a unique moment, and that moment cannot be repeated. The archive classification system is developed to record every specimen produced.

2021

The First Commission

A client commissions a series of twelve pieces from a specific garden — her late mother's garden in Devon — to be collected and pressed before the property is sold. The commission establishes the framework for all subsequent commissioned work: site visit, botanical survey, collection schedule aligned with seasonal flowering, and full documentation. The twelve Devon pieces are among the most significant work the atelier has produced.

2024

International Field Work

HERBA CARTA begins collecting outside the UK for the first time — a two-week field trip to the Alentejo region of Portugal during spring flowering. The resulting collection of 28 specimens, all documented with GPS coordinates and meteorological data from the collection date, is the largest single collection the atelier has produced. Twelve pieces from the Alentejo collection are available through the current catalogue.
