The KIRI Method

The Ritual

Philosophy of Service

"The ritual of sake is inseparable from its taste. How you are poured for shapes what you receive. We take this seriously."

At KIRI, service is not a transaction but a choreography — planned in advance, responsive in the moment, and designed above all to put your senses in the best possible position to receive what the sake has to offer. Every element of how we serve has been considered against the question: does this help or hinder genuine attention?

The Five Movements

From Arrival
to Close

Movement 01

The Arrival

You are greeted by your Sakagura guide and seated without rush. No menus are presented immediately. A brief conversation establishes your preferences, your experience level, and your intention for the evening.

Movement 02

The Opening Pour

We begin always with something gentle — a cold, clean junmai or ginjo that recalibrates your palate without demanding too much. This is a question, not a statement.

Movement 03

The Journey

Across four to six pours, the selection moves deliberately: from clean to complex, cold to warm, young to aged. Each transition is guided by your responses and the seasonal context.

Movement 04

The Pairing

Seasonal Japanese food is offered between selected pours — not to fill the appetite, but to demonstrate how sake and food complete each other's silences.

Movement 05

The Close

The final pour is always warm, always weighty — often an aged koshu of significant character. Green tea closes the session. The silence that follows is part of the meal.

Key Ingredients

What Makes
Great Sake

Yamada Nishiki Rice

Hyogo Prefecture

The undisputed king of sake rice — large, starchy grains that polish beautifully and produce fragrant, complex junmai daiginjo.

Mountain Spring Water

Various Prefectures

Water mineral content defines regional style: soft Kyoto water for delicate ginjo, hard Nada water for assertive, structured sake.

Koji Mold

Aspergillus oryzae

The alchemical heart of sake — koji converts rice starch to fermentable sugars. Its cultivation is the most skilled and closely guarded step in the brewing process.

Kimoto Starter

Traditional method

The ancient kimoto method of lactic acid fermentation — laboriously stirred by hand — produces sake of extraordinary structure and longevity.
