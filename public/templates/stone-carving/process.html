How Stone Becomes Sculpture

Process

— The Method

Stone carving at this level is not a sequence of tasks — it is a sustained conversation between the carver and the material. Each step requires patience, judgement, and the willingness to stop when the stone requires it. What follows is the honest account of how we work.

— Five Stages

From Commission to Completion

I
Written Correspondence

Every commission begins with a letter — no forms, no telephone calls. We ask the client to describe, in their own words, what they wish to create and why it should be made in stone. This is not a formality. It tells us everything we need to know about whether the commission is one we can accept.

II
Stone Selection at Quarry

If we accept the commission, we travel to the quarry. We walk the stone yards. We assess density, crystalline grain, veining, and geological age. We sometimes leave empty-handed and return. We do not accept a block that does not speak to the commission. The stone selection is the first act of carving.

III
Model and Study

For figurative work, we work from life sessions when possible — twelve or more studio visits with the subject. For architectural work, we build 1:10 clay models and, for large commissions, full-scale cardboard templates. Nothing is assumed. Proportion is verified at every scale before stone is touched.

IV
The Carving — Point to Finish

The carving sequence follows a discipline unchanged for centuries: point chisel to rough form; claw chisel to develop planes; flat chisel to refine surface; riffler and rasp to approach final form; abrasives through decreasing grit to the finished surface. Each tool removed applies knowledge the previous one could not deliver.

V
Inspection, Delivery, and Installation

We inspect the completed work under raking light — the only honest light for stone surface evaluation. Final adjustments are made in the studio. We deliver and install every work ourselves. We do not hand a sculpture to a shipping company and consider the commission complete. The work is finished when it is in its intended place.

— The Stones

Materials We Work

Carrara Statuario
Metamorphic Marble · Triassic Period · Apuan Alps

The stone of Michelangelo. Maximum translucency at 35mm depth. Reserved for portrait and figurative work requiring skin luminosity.

Lasa Bianco
Metamorphic Marble · Palaeozoic · Val Venosta

The purest white marble known. Near-zero crystalline grain. Preferred for portraits requiring absolute clarity of surface.

Sardinian Granite
Igneous Granite · Carboniferous · Barbagia

For outdoor architectural commissions requiring permanence of millennia. Crystalline density 2.7 g/cm³.

Black Schist
Metamorphic · Various · On Commission

Used selectively for abstract work and memorial pieces. The foliation of schist introduces directional character unique in stone carving.

— Tools of the Trade

The Instruments

We use hand tools. No CNC routing, no robotic carving, no mechanical reproduction of any kind. This is not a philosophical position held for its own sake — it is a technical reality. Hand carving produces a surface quality and a responsive sensitivity to the stone's internal character that no machine can approach.

Our tools are made to order by a blacksmith in Pietrasanta who has supplied Carraran carvers for three generations. Point chisels, claw chisels, flat chisels, rifflers, rasps, and pneumatic air chisels for large-scale work — every tool a direct extension of the hand.

The pneumatic air chisel, used carefully on granite, allows us to execute architectural work at scale without loss of control. It is the one concession to mechanisation we make — and we use it sparingly.
