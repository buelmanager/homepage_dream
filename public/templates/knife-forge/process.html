The Forge Process

How a Blade is Made

From Stock to Edge

Every blade Marcus Volker makes passes through the same five stages. There are no shortcuts between them, no stages that can be combined, and no point at which the blade can be recovered if a judgement is wrong. Steel does not forgive carelessness.

01
Stock Selection & Profiling

The commission brief determines the steel. Each alloy has a different heat response, edge geometry, and toughness profile. Marcus profiles the blade with an angle grinder and belt grinder — removing everything that is not the blade before the steel ever sees fire.

02
Forge Work

The steel enters the forge at 1,100–1,250°C depending on the alloy. Marcus works with a 75kg power hammer and by hand with a cross-peen hammer. Forging aligns the grain structure of the steel — not necessary for stock removal, but irreplaceable for final edge performance.

03
Normalising & Annealing

Three normalising cycles bring the steel back to a controlled grain state. A slow anneal in the cooling forge reduces internal stress. The blade is now rough-shaped, soft, and stable — ready for final grinding.

04
Hardening & Tempering

The blade is brought to critical temperature and quenched — in oil, water, or interrupted quench depending on the steel and the commission. Then tempered: twice, minimum. This is the moment that cannot be undone. Marcus has retried one quench in thirty years. He was unhappy with the colour of the steel before it went in.

05
Final Grinding, Handle Work & Edge

The hardened blade is ground to final geometry on ceramic belts. Handle scales are fitted, drilled, pinned, and shaped by hand. The edge is set on waterstones — progression through 400, 1000, 3000, and 8000 grit. Marcus tests every edge on paper before it leaves the forge.
