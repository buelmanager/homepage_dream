Seven Hours in Copper

Distillation

The Method

One distillation per year. One copper pot still. Seven hours from first vapour to final cut. Nothing blended. Nothing adjusted. The gin is what the autumn gave us, rendered honest through copper.

The Process
01
The Foraging Window

Between the first morning frost and the autumn equinox, a team of six foragers walks the Silvardin Reserve. Juniper is taken from the north-facing ridge where oil concentration peaks at first frost. Elderflower from the loch margin at dawn. Each botanical has a precise window, measured in days, sometimes hours.

02
Sorting and Assessment

Every botanical is hand-sorted on arrival at the distillery. Damaged or under-ripe material is discarded. Each botanical is assessed for aromatic density by crushing and smelling — no instruments, just the distiller's nose, trained across twelve harvests. Only material at peak aromatic density enters the still.

03
Maceration

Botanicals are macerated overnight in neutral grain spirit in the copper alembic. The maceration period is determined by the botanical composition of each year — heavier floral years require less contact time. The contact duration is not fixed in any recipe. It is decided each year by tasting.

04
The Seven-Hour Run

The 250-litre Portuguese copper alembic runs once. The distillation takes seven hours from flame to final drop. The heads are discarded. The tails are set aside for further analysis. The heart — the two-hour window that constitutes the finished gin — is collected by Ailsa alone. No assistant handles the cut.

05
Rest and Bottling

The heart rests for three weeks in glass demijohns at cellar temperature. It is tasted blind against previous harvests on day 21. If approved, it is diluted to 44% ABV with local spring water and bottled by hand — 312 bottles, each numbered by hand, each carrying the harvest coordinates and the date of distillation.

The Seven Botanicals
Juniper
Juniperus communis · Highland Ridge · 57.24°N

The spine. Foraged at first frost from the north-facing slope where oil peaks. Resinous and bright.

Coriander Seed
Coriandrum sativum · Bulgarian Highlands

Warm, earthy, anchoring. The only non-local botanical. Sourced from a single farm in the Rhodope mountains.

Elderflower
Sambucus nigra · Loch Margin · 57.19°N

Picked at dawn before the dew lifts. Soft, muscat-floral. The gin's exhale.

Cucumber Peel
Cucumis sativus · Distillery Garden

Grown in the distillery garden. A breath of cool water that prevents the juniper from closing in.

Green Cardamom
Elettaria cardamomum · Kerala Spice Coast

The deep note that lingers. Camphor-eucalyptus over warm pepper. Only reveals itself on the finish.

The Still

A 250-Litre Portuguese Alembic

The Silvardin still was installed in 2017 and has never been replaced. It is a traditional alembic — a pot still with a copper swan neck and a hand-beaten copper onion head. The shape of the still determines the character of the distillate. This still produces a round, full spirit with exceptional floral retention.

The conversation between botanical vapour and copper is chemistry. Copper ions react with sulphur compounds, removing harsh notes that would otherwise survive into the gin. The seven-hour run is not theatrical — it is the minimum time required for the botanicals to fully surrender their character to the spirit.

The still runs once per year. In the twelve months between distillations, it is cleaned, inspected, and left in silence. Ready for the next autumn.
