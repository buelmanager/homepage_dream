The Process

The Urushi Method

Five stages that cannot be rushed

Urushi lacquer cures through a chemical reaction triggered by humidity and heat. You cannot speed it. You cannot simulate it. You can only wait, and apply the next layer when the previous one is ready to receive it. URWA pieces require a minimum of six months of active production. Complex commissions take three years or more.

一
Wood Preparation
木地固め — Kiji-katame

The wood substrate — cypress, zelkova, or cherry selected for the commission — is dried for a minimum of one year before work begins. Then ground with urushi thinned to penetrate the wood grain, sealing it before any surface layering begins.

Duration: 2–4 weeks · Humidity chamber: 80% RH, 25°C
二
Base Lacquer Layers
下地 — Shitaji

A series of twelve to twenty base layers is applied, each cured in the lacquering chamber, each sanded with progressively finer stones until the surface is level. These layers are invisible in the finished work. They determine everything about it.

Duration: 6–12 weeks · Each layer: 24–48 hours cure time
三
Middle & Colour Layers
中塗り — Nakanuri

The middle layers establish the colour and opacity of the finished work. For red pieces, we may apply a base of bengara (iron oxide) urushi before the cinnabar surface. For black, carbon black mixed into the urushi creates a depth that no paint can produce.

Duration: 8–16 weeks · 15–30 additional layers
四
Surface Layers & Decoration
上塗り — Uwanuri & 蒔絵 — Maki-e

The final surface layers are applied with a brush made from human hair — the only material that carries urushi with the required smoothness. If maki-e decoration is specified, gold, silver, or coloured powder is sifted through a bamboo tube onto the wet lacquer in precise patterns.

Duration: 4–8 weeks · Maki-e: add 4–12 weeks
五
Polishing & Completion
研出し — Togidashi

The final polishing of a URWA piece takes between twenty and forty hours. We begin with charcoal blocks of graded fineness, move to raw deer hide, and finish with the ball of the palm. The final surface is never touched by machine. We deliver it wrapped in washi paper in a paulownia box.

Duration: 2–4 weeks · The longest stage per object
The Instruments

Tools that have not changed in 400 years

Our lacquering brushes, chisels, and polishing stones are the same instruments used in Kyoto ateliers since the Edo period. We do not adopt new tools without a minimum three-year trial period.

刷
Urushi Brush — 漆刷毛

Made from human hair, bound with washi, set in a bamboo handle. Our senior lacquerer has used the same brush for eleven years. It improves with each use.

砥
Polishing Stones — 砥石

Togashishi charcoal from Wakayama Prefecture, graded from #400 to #10000. Each stone is used wet, in one direction only, on a plane that has been set with a granite reference.

筒
Maki-e Tube — 蒔絵筒

A hollow bamboo tube with a fine silk membrane at the base, used to sift gold and silver powder onto wet lacquer. The membrane fineness determines the powder density.
