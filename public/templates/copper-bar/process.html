Process

How a Verdigris Drink is Made

The chemistry comes first.
The drink follows.

Every cocktail on the Verdigris menu begins with a chemical question, not a flavour preference. What does copper do to this compound? What happens when this acid meets this tannin at this temperature? What is the optimal pH for this botanical to express its volatile esters? The answers produce the drinks. The flavour is a consequence of the chemistry, not the starting point.

Five Stages
01
Stage One

Research and Hypothesis

Each new drink begins with a research phase. Elena identifies a chemical interaction that interests her — a particular pH effect, an oxidative pathway, a copper compound — and frames it as a hypothesis: if this ingredient is processed in this way, this flavour result should follow. The hypothesis is tested using the lab's analytical equipment before any tasting session is convened. Most hypotheses are wrong. The ones that are right become drinks.

Duration: 2–6 weeks per drink concept
02
Stage Two

Distillation and Preparation

Verdigris produces a range of house distillates in its 40-litre alembic still. The still is Portuguese copper, purchased secondhand from a farmhouse distillery in the Douro Valley. All botanical selections for house gin production are sourced from single suppliers and assayed before distillation. Additional preparations — fat washes, acid solutions, tannin reductions — are produced in the lab kitchen adjacent to the still room.

Duration: 1–3 days per distillation run
03
Stage Three

Calibration

Before any drink is added to the menu, it undergoes a calibration process. This involves measuring the pH of every acidic component, recording the Brix of every sweet element, testing the drink's temperature curve over twenty minutes, and assessing how its character changes between the first and last sip. A drink that tastes better at the beginning than the end is rejected. The ideal Verdigris cocktail improves for the first fifteen minutes of service.

Duration: 1–2 weeks · Multiple calibration sessions
04
Stage Four

Service Design

Every drink has a designed service format. This includes the vessel — glassware, copper cup, terracotta, or other material — the temperature at which it is served, whether it is stirred or built to order or prepared in batch, the optimal drinking pace, and any tableside elements. The service instructions are written down and part of the staff briefing. How a drink is served is as important as what is in it.

Duration: 1 week · Service protocol testing
05
Stage Five

Quarterly Review

The cocktail menu is rebuilt from scratch every quarter. Drinks from the previous menu are retired regardless of popularity — the only exception is if a core ingredient is no longer available and the drink cannot honestly be made. The quarterly review is not an event. It is a standing practice, built into the bar's schedule and Elena's working calendar. No menu has ever been extended by demand. No drink has ever returned after retirement.

Duration: 4 weeks · Full menu reconstruction per quarter
Key Ingredients

What copper
does to a drink

Copper affects every stage of drink production that passes through it. As a still material, it removes sulphur compounds from spirit vapour — a chemical reaction that makes copper-distilled spirits cleaner and more aromatic than those produced in stainless. As a vessel material, it continues to react with organic compounds in slow, subtle ways. As a surface, it develops a patina that is a record of everything that has touched it.

  • House Gin — Copper Pot Distilled
    Produced in the 40-litre alembic. Botanicals selected for their behaviour in copper contact. Current expression uses fourteen botanicals, four of which are selected specifically because they react measurably with copper during distillation.
  • Copper-Rested Vermouth
    Commercial dry vermouth stored in a copper flask for 72 hours before use in copper-series drinks. The contact produces a measurable change in the wine's volatile esters — Elena calls it "making the vermouth honest."
  • Calibrated Acid Solutions
    All sour components in Verdigris cocktails are produced in-house by dissolving food-grade citric, malic, or tartaric acid to measured pH levels. No lemon juice is used — too variable. Acids are measured fresh each evening.
  • Copper-Water Syrups
    Sugar syrups made using water that has been filtered through a copper mesh column. The copper transfers trace mineral content that affects the syrup's perceived sweetness and mouthfeel without any detectable metallic flavour.
The Still

The 40-Litre Portuguese Alembic

Verdigris's still was purchased in 2019 from a retired farmhouse distillery in the Douro Valley. It is a traditional alembic design, hand-hammered from 2mm copper sheet by a Portuguese coppersmith who no longer makes stills. The column is a swan-neck type with a worm condenser of approximately 4 metres of coiled copper tubing. It produces approximately 8 litres of distillate per run.

The still is cleaned with steam and hot water after every run. No chemical cleaning agents are used. The interior of the pot has developed a thin copper oxide layer over five years of use that Elena regards as a contribution to the spirit's character.

Capacity
40 L
Output
~8 L/run
Origin
Douro, 1994

The Lab Kitchen

Adjacent to the still room, the lab kitchen is where all non-distilled preparations are produced: fat washes, acid solutions, tannin reductions, infusions, and batch preparations for high-volume serves. It contains two induction hobs, a centrifuge, a Brix refractometer, and a pH meter calibrated before each evening service.
